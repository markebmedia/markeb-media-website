Discovery Call Sales Playbook

A consultative guide for BDMs — helping clients work out what's right for them, not pushing them towards a package.

UseDiscovery & Opening Calls

This playbook is a guide, not a script

The goal on a discovery call is to understand what the client actually needs — then help them see which service fits that need. You're an adviser, not a salesperson. The best outcome is a client who feels confident they've made the right choice, not one who's been closed on a package.

01

Listen first

Before introducing any service, understand the client's current situation — what they're doing now, what's working, and where they feel there's a gap.

02

Ask, don't assume

Use the discovery questions in each service card to surface what they actually need. Their answers tell you where to go — not the other way around.

03

Match, don't push

When you introduce a service, connect it directly to something they've told you. If it doesn't fit their situation, say so — that honesty builds far more trust than a close.

04

Let the logic do the work

The pricing and packaging make sense on their own terms. Once a client understands what's included and why, they'll reach the right decision themselves.

Discovery call flow

A suggested structure for opening calls. This isn't a script — adapt it to how the conversation naturally develops. The goal is to spend the first half of the call listening, and only introduce services once you understand their situation.

1

Open with context, not a pitch

Start by understanding who they are and what they do — agency size, types of properties, how they currently market listings. This isn't small talk; it tells you which services are even relevant.

"Before I talk through what we offer, it'd be useful to understand a bit more about how you're currently approaching property marketing — what does a typical listing campaign look like for you at the moment?"
2

Find the gap

Once you understand their current setup, ask what's not working or what they wish they had more of. The gap they describe tells you exactly which service to introduce.

"Is there anything about your current marketing that you feel isn't doing the properties justice — or something vendors ask for that you can't currently deliver?"
3

Introduce the relevant service

Connect the service directly to what they've just told you. Don't introduce everything — only what's relevant to their situation. Use the consultative framing in the service cards to frame it as a question, not a statement.

"Based on what you've said about wanting to be more active on social, it might be worth looking at how video could work for you — are you open to exploring that?"
4

Walk through the logic, not the features

When you explain a service, lead with what it means for the client — not what it includes. Use the "why this price" copy to justify value, not defend cost.

"The reason the Silver Package tends to be where most agents land is that it covers all the channels in one visit — stills for Rightmove, video for social, aerial for both. You're not going back and forth booking different services for different properties."
5

Let them decide — don't close

Once you've introduced the relevant service and walked through the logic, give them space. Ask whether it makes sense for their situation. If they're not ready, find out what would help them feel more confident.

"Does that feel like the right fit for what you're trying to do, or is there something about it that doesn't quite sit right?"

Handling objections

Most objections aren't objections — they're requests for more information or reassurance. The goal is never to overcome them, but to understand what's behind them and respond honestly.

Price
It's more than we usually spend on photography.
That's a fair point to raise. It's worth understanding what you're currently getting for what you spend — and whether it's delivering what you need. Our pricing reflects professional-grade equipment, specialist travel, and post-production editing that makes a genuine difference to how a property presents. If budget is a constraint, it's worth starting with what would make the biggest difference to your listings and working from there. What's the one thing you feel your current photography is missing?
Commitment
We're not sure we need this for every listing.
That's completely reasonable — and honestly, not every property needs the same level of content. It's worth thinking about which properties in your portfolio would benefit most from professional photography or video, and starting there. We can work with you on a per-property basis rather than a blanket approach if that makes more sense for how you operate.
Relevance
Our vendors don't really ask for video.
That's interesting — do you think that's because they don't see it as an option, or because they genuinely don't value it? In our experience, vendors rarely ask for video specifically, but they respond very positively when they see it. It also tends to benefit the agent as much as the vendor — it's content you can use for your own marketing, not just the listing.
Timing
We'd like to think about it and come back to you.
Of course — it's not a decision to rush. It'd help to know what would make it easier to come to a decision. Is there something specific you'd want to think through, or something I haven't covered that would help? I'm happy to send something over in writing so you've got it to refer back to.
Competition
We've had cheaper quotes from other photographers.
It's worth comparing like for like — what's included, what the editing looks like, and what the turnaround is. We're not the cheapest option, and we wouldn't want to be. If price is the main factor, we might not be the right fit. But if the quality of output and reliability of the service matters — which for most agents it does — it's worth looking at what the difference actually buys you.
Personal branding
I'm not sure I'm comfortable on camera.
That's one of the most common things we hear — and it's exactly why the session is directed. You're not left to figure it out yourself. Our specialists guide you through the whole thing, and most people find they're far more comfortable than they expected once they're in it. Would it help to talk through what the session actually looks like in practice?

Principles

The mindset behind every successful discovery call. Come back to these when a call isn't going the way you'd hoped.

Listen more than you speak

The first half of any discovery call should be mostly the client talking. You're gathering information, not delivering a presentation. The more they say, the clearer the right service becomes.

Connect before you introduce

Never introduce a service without first connecting it to something the client has told you. If you can't make that connection, it's probably not the right service for them right now.

Honesty closes faster than pressure

If a service isn't the right fit, say so. Clients who feel advised rather than sold to are far more likely to come back — and to refer others. Short-term honesty is always the right long-term move.

The packaging logic does the selling

The services are priced and structured to make sense on their own terms. Your job is to explain the logic clearly — not to persuade. A client who understands what they're getting and why it costs what it does will make the right decision themselves.

A no now can be a yes later

Not every call ends in a booking — and that's fine. A client who leaves feeling respected and well-informed will come back when the time is right. Focus on the relationship, not the transaction.
